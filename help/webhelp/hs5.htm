Flatness, Symmetry, Area Ratio, Elevation and Linac symmetry Error
general description 


Definitions for flatness and symmetry
The Absolute flatness is defined as maximum-minimum, i.e. the vertical range, denoted by the two red lines below. This value is presented for a filtered measurement and limited to the In-Field area, indicated by the vertical blue lines below. The In-Field area concept is based on conventional profiles. The same range however is still useful for non-flat profiles and then should be seen as analysis area. When a division of two curves is evaluated, the relative flatness as calculated from the blue "calculated" curve.
For FFF profiles the the flatness is meaningless. When a such a profile is detected the flatness is annotated with (F) and expressed in percent per cm. When both absolute and relative flatness are given for a FFF-curve, the absolute flatness is followed by a "c" and must be read as "%/cm".
The symmetry is defined as the largest ratio maximum[centre-x]-minimum[center+x] for a point and its mirrored position within the In-Field area as calculated on a filtered curve found at the red arrows below.
These definitions are based on the Dutch NCS protocol and comply with most international protocols.

Elevation
When all measurement points within the In-Field area are fed into a linear (first degree) model, the slope is a good measure for the symmetry as shown in the example above. The slope is recalculated to a normalised height difference over the In-Field area: 100·slope·(In-Field area width) / (normalisation value). In the example above this is represented by the red arrow.


Area Ratio

The area ratio is defined as 2·(right half In-Field area - left half In-Field area) / (total In-Field area). This alternative measure for the symmetry is insensitive to noise, has signed result value and gives a clinically more relevant figure. There is a caveat however with the definition given here when the field is asymmetrical with respect to the field center. A asymmetrical range will then produce large effects with a perfectly flat field. Also a asymmetric distribution of the data points can have significant effects for small fields. Therefore a resampled, symmetrical range (with the length of the In-Field area) is taken around the choosen field center. The center of field might be the origin, the middle of the field borders or the top of a FFF field. In the example above the actual calculation is 2*(B - A) / (A + B). Since the center is significantly different from the origin, the area ratio value is annotated with a 'b'.


Linac symmetry error

The internal ionization chamber of a linac compares the signal of two symmetric regions to evaluate the symmetry of the beam. For an Elekta machine segments of a ring shaped area with a inner radius of 7.3 cm (at isoc) and an outer diameter of about 15.3 cm, indicated with the green boxes below. By definition, the signal is averaged within each box ("L" and "R"). The Linac symmetry error is now calculated as 2*(L-R)/(L+R). For the calculation in BistroMath the evaluation is also limited by the field borders (the 50% positions or derivative based). Thus for smaller fields the Linac symmetry error possibly is not calculated at all. The boxes are corrected for actual SSD and measurement depth. It is displayed when the Linac symmetry error is selected in the Measurement menu. The inner and outer radius can be set in the Settings tab.


Relative flatness

When there is a reference available, a division of measurement and reference can be calculated. The relative flatness is found as maximum and minimum within the In-Field area for this line.
